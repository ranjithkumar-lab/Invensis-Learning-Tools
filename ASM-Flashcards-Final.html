What Does a Scrum Master Actually Do? A 2026 Guide

Invensis Learning Team
Certified Scrum trainers
Updated July 2026 8 min read Agile · ASM
🌀

"Scrum Master" is one of the most misunderstood roles in a software team. Some people picture a project manager with a new title. Others assume it is just the person who runs the daily stand-up. The reality is more interesting, and knowing exactly what the role covers is essential for anyone preparing for the Agile Scrum Master (ASM) certification.

In this guide we will walk through the responsibilities, ceremonies, and mindset that define a real Scrum Master. At the end you can test your recall on the key ASM concepts with our free flashcard tool.

1. A servant leader, not a boss

A Scrum Master does not assign tasks or approve holidays. They serve the team. Their job is to make the team more effective by facilitating conversations, removing obstacles, and shielding the team from outside pressure. Good Scrum Masters lead through influence and coaching, not authority.

"The Scrum Master is accountable for the effectiveness of Scrum itself, not the delivery of the product."

2. Facilitating the Scrum events

Every Sprint runs through four core events, and the Scrum Master ensures each one happens well:

  • Sprint Planning: the team commits to a Sprint Goal and picks work from the backlog.
  • Daily Scrum: a 15-minute daily sync where the team inspects progress toward the goal.
  • Sprint Review: the team shows the Increment to stakeholders and gathers feedback.
  • Sprint Retrospective: the team reflects on how it worked and plans improvements.

3. Coaching the team, the Product Owner, and the organisation

The role expands outward in three concentric circles. Coach the team on self-management and technical practices. Support the Product Owner in backlog refinement and stakeholder communication. Help the wider organisation understand and embrace empirical, Agile ways of working.

    4. What a Scrum Master is not

    A Scrum Master is not a Project Manager. They do not commit to dates on the team's behalf, they do not assign tasks, and they do not report on individual performance. If you find yourself doing those things, you have drifted out of the role. Come back to the question: How can I make this team more effective at Scrum?
